Conceptual bridge: keep the phase difference, change the cause

Page 1 physically slides the red source to expose the phase shift. In many real interference situations, however, both coherent sources remain fixed. The same phase shift appears because the waves travel different distances—and therefore take different times—to reach a detector.

How to use:

  • Slider: Moves detector D while S1 and S2 remain fixed.
  • Integer Δ/λ: the waves arrive in phase—crest meets crest.
  • Half-integer Δ/λ: the waves arrive out of phase—crest meets trough.
Teacher explanation: bridge Page 1 to Page 2

Name the representational change: Page 1 deliberately moves the red source so students can see a wave shifted by Δ. Page 2 keeps S1 and S2 fixed. Here the lower graph compares the phases arriving at D; it does not show either source moving.

  1. End Page 1 at Δ = 0.50λ. Ask students to explain why crest meets trough when one starting point is shifted by half a wavelength.
  2. Open Page 2 at Δ = 0. D is equally far from S1 and S2, so both waves spend the same time travelling. Ask: “How could we create the same half-cycle delay without moving a source?”
  3. Hide the resultant and move D slowly. Moving D lengthens one path and shortens the other. The sources have not changed; the waves now take different times to reach D.
  4. Stop at Δ = 0.50λ. One wave has travelled half a wavelength farther, so it arrives half a cycle later: crest meets trough. Ask students to predict the resultant before revealing it.
  5. Compare Δ = 1.00λ and 1.50λ. Build the rules from evidence: Δ = nλ gives constructive interference; Δ = (n + ½)λ gives destructive interference.
  6. Animate last. Emphasise that interference is continuous superposition at D—not a one-time collision of two drawn curves.
